Some terms to start

A few terms to begin. The bride is called the Kallah, and the groom is called the Chattan (the ch is pronounced like the ch in loch). A Jewish wedding takes place under a canopy called the Chuppah. The Chuppah is only covered on the top, this symbolises a fusion of the home but also of openness.

The Ceremony

The day will start with a Tisch, which means “table” in Yiddish/German. The bridegroom, family, men, and women, will sing and drink/eat whilst the Ketubah is being prepared.

The Ketubah is an ancient 2nd Century marriage prenup written in Aramaic, the language of the Jewish community of Babylon (c. 500 BC). It is a contract of commitment between the man and the woman, and is signed confirmed by two witnesses.

Once the contract is signed and everyone is singing, the Tisch-goers will go down to the ceremony, and everyone will be seated. The Chattan walks in with his parents and waits for his Kallah under the Chuppah.

He will need to confirm the bride is who he thinks she is. This is because of the story of Jacob in the Bible. Jacob got tricked by a scheming father-in-law to marry Leah instead of his preferred choice Rachel. This process is called the Bedecken, The Chattan will confirm it is his wife to be and lower her veil.

The Rabbi will introduce the couple and start the marriage process. The Ketubah will be read out, and will be followed by a series of seven blessings over a cup of wine read out by male relatives and close friends. The wine is then drunk by the Kallah and the Chattan, there is the potential for major wedding dress ruining here, which explains the choice of white over red wine.

Then the Chattan will utter the following line whilst giving the Kallah a ring, “With this item I make you holy you to me”. After that a glass will be broken and all hell breaks loose. The glass is broken to symbolise the destruction of the Jewish Temple in Jerusalem, which occurred in 72CE, around 40 years after Jesus. According to Jewish tradition, the Jewish people is in a state of exile until the Temple is rebuilt.

The Party

After the Chuppah, the couple are whisked away to a room for time to recover. Meanwhile, there’s a reception, followed by the party. Jewish weddings have a lot of dancing, so bring comfortable shoes! Below is a playlist with some of the songs to get you in the mood, it's going to be fun!
