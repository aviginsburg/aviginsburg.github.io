Yael and Avi's Wedding

19 June 2019 ☕️☕️☕️ 4 min read

Hey friend! Soon you will be dancing with us at maybe your first Jewish wedding. Good luck, it is going to be pretty heavy. A lot of stuff is going to happen, so this will be your guide to the day.

A Jewish wedding, like many other religious weddings, is a day of the union of two people. Much will look familiar from a traditional Christian ceremony, but there are a number of key differences. Another thing to note is that Jewish tradition is reasonably patriarchal, so a number of things throughout the day will seem a tad dated. These have been modernised slightly, but this isn’t an apology.

Some terms to start!

A few terms to begin. The bride is called the Kalah, and the groom is called the Chattan. Also, the wedding takes place under an awning called the Chuppah.

The Ceremony

A Jewish wedding is a mixture of Halacha and Minhag. The day will start with a Tisch, which means table in Yiddish, but is when the men (and some women depending on the ceremony), will sing and drink/eat whilst the Ketubah is being prepared.

The Ketubah is an ancient 2nd Century marriage prenup. It is a list of things the Chattan is required to provide for his wife, with a failure to meet these being grounds for divorce. These include shelter, food, and good sex. Fun right? The Ketubah is written in Aramaic, a language spoken by a few Iraqi tribes in Northern Iraq and the language of the Jewish community of Babylon (c. 500 BC), and is signed by two male witnesses (we will come back to it later).

Once the contract is signed and everyone is singing (there is a whole section on signing), the Tisch-goers will go down to the ceremony, and everyone will be seated. The Chattan will need to confirm the bride is who he thinks she is. this is because of the story of Jacob in the Bible. Jacob got tricked by a scheming father-in-law to marry Leah instead of his preferred choice Rebecca. Luckily for me, Yael’s sister is married but it is always good to be certain #longlivethepatricarchy.

This process is called the Bedeken, The Chattan will lift up the veil of the Kalah, confirm it is her and then put it down again. The Bedeken can occur in a number of different places, but for this wedding it will occur when the Kalah walks down to the Chuppah.

The Rabbi (there’s no definition for Rabbi, you should know what this is), will introduce the couple and start the marriage process. The Ketubah will be read out, enjoy (very long and not massively interesting). There will be a series of seven blessings over a cup of wine read out by male relatives and close friends #yep. The wine is then drunk by the Kalah and the Chattan, there is the potential for major wedding dress ruining here, so this is done extremely carefully.

Then the couple will utter the following line whilst giving the utter party a ring, “With this item I consecrate you to me”. After that a glass will be broken and all hell breaks loose. The glass is broken to symbolise the destruction of the Jewish Temple in Jerusalem, which occurred in 72CE, around 40 years after Jesus was killed. Jews are not complete until the Temple is rebuilt.

The Party

After the Chuppah, the couple are whisked away to a room for *cough* time to recover. During this time, the room is guarded by the two witnesses who signed the Ketubah.

After the reception, the dancing starts. Jewish weddings have a lot of dancing! Our party will be divided into three sections; separate dancing, mixed Horah dancing. and then party music.
